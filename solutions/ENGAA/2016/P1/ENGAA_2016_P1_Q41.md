## Worked solution

The lines are perpendicular, so

\[
p=-\frac{1}{m}.
\]

The \(x\)-intercept of \(y=mx+3\) is

\[
-\frac{3}{m}.
\]

The \(x\)-intercept of \(y=px+2\) is

\[
-\frac{2}{p}=2m.
\]

The distance between the intercepts is \(5\), so

\[
2m+\frac{3}{m}=5.
\]

Multiplying by \(m\) gives

\[
2m^{2}-5m+3=0.
\]

Thus

\[
m=1
\quad\text{or}\quad
m=\frac{3}{2}.
\]

Since \(m>1\) and \(m\ne1\), use \(m=\frac{3}{2}\). Then

\[
p=-\frac{2}{3}
\]

and

\[
m+p=\frac{3}{2}-\frac{2}{3}=\frac{5}{6}.
\]

## Quickest method

Use perpendicular gradients and the two \(x\)-intercepts.

## Common trap

Keeping the excluded root \(m=1\), or using signed displacement instead of distance.
